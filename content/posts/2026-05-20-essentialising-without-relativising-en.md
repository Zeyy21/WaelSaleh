The study of political Islam is too often caught between two reflexes. The first essentialises: it treats *Islam*, *culture*, or *civilisation* as fixed substances from which political behaviour follows almost mechanically. The second relativises: in its anxiety to avoid the first error, it suspends critique altogether, as though analysing an ideology were the same as condemning a people.

Neither reflex helps us understand anything. Both substitute a posture for an argument.

## Analysing ideas, not identities

The wager of my work is that we can do better by shifting the object of analysis. The unit of study is not a community but a **discourse** — a structured set of claims, justifications, and silences that does political work in the world. To ask how a movement legitimises violence, how it constructs an internal *Other*, or how it migrates onto digital platforms is to ask empirical questions with empirical answers.

> Critique without essentialising, deconstruct without relativising, dialogue without surrendering analytical rigour.

This is not a rhetorical balance. It is a methodological discipline, and it has consequences for what counts as evidence, for whom we cite, and for which questions we are even able to pose.

## What follows

In the entries that follow I want to test these commitments against concrete cases — the representation of non-Islamist Muslims in Islamist texts, the epistemology of radicalisation studies, and the strange afterlife of the post-Arab-Spring moment. The aim is not to settle these questions but to show what becomes visible once we refuse both essentialism and relativism.
